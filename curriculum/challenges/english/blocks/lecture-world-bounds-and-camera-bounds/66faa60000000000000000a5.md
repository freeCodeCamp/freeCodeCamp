# --interactive--

World bounds are about *physics*. **Camera bounds** are about what the player sees. They are independent. Setting the world bounds tells the simulation how large the level is; setting the camera bounds tells the camera where it is allowed to scroll.

```js
this.cameras.main.setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240);
```

After that call, no matter how far the followed target travels, the camera's left edge cannot drop below 0 and the right edge cannot exceed 4000. The camera *clamps* to the rectangle — exactly the behaviour you want for almost every traditional level.

:::interactive_editor

```js
const camera = { x: 0, width: 320 };
const bounds = { x: 0, width: 4000 };
const player = { x: 3950 };

function update() {
  let desired = player.x - camera.width / 2;
  const maxX = bounds.x + bounds.width - camera.width;
  const minX = bounds.x;
  if (desired > maxX) desired = maxX;
  if (desired < minX) desired = minX;
  camera.x = desired;
}

update();
console.log("camera near right edge:", camera.x);

player.x = 10;
update();
console.log("camera near left edge:", camera.x);
```

:::

Without bounds, the camera can scroll past the playable area. The result is unsightly: at the start of the level the player appears in the middle of the screen, but half the screen is showing empty world coordinates outside the level. Setting camera bounds equal to the world size keeps the camera *inside the world*.

Camera bounds also interact with `roundPixels`:

```js
this.cameras.main.setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240).setRoundPixels(true);
```

`setRoundPixels(true)` snaps the camera scroll to whole pixels, which avoids subpixel sampling artifacts on pixel art. For HD art, leave it off — subpixel precision makes lerp look smoother.

A nuance: camera bounds, like world bounds, can be smaller than the visible canvas. If the canvas is 320 wide and the camera bounds are 200 wide, the camera centres the bounds on the canvas and you see the world plus padding on either side. Phaser does not stretch the camera; it just shows you the world rectangle.

The `cameras.main.setBounds` call returns the camera, so it chains:

```js
this.cameras.main
  .setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)
  .startFollow(this.player, true, 0.1, 0.1)
  .setDeadzone(200, 100);
```

That's the standard cinematic-camera setup in three lines.

# --questions--

## --text--

Why does the camera scroll past the playable area when you only set the world bounds?

## --answers--

World bounds are physics-only. The camera's scroll behaviour is governed by `cameras.main.setBounds`, which is independent.

---

Because the player's body is too small.

### --feedback--

The camera does not look at the body's size; it looks at its own bounds.

---

Because `startFollow` ignores world bounds by default.

### --feedback--

`startFollow` does not look at world bounds at all — that's intentional. Camera bounds are a separate axis.

---

Because the canvas is the wrong aspect ratio.

### --feedback--

Aspect ratio does not factor into camera scroll bounds. The two systems are independent.

## --text--

You call `cameras.main.setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)`, but the camera still scrolls past `x = 4000`. What's most likely?

## --answers--

You called `setBounds` *after* `startFollow` and an earlier scroll position is being preserved — call `setBounds` first, or call `setScroll` to clamp manually.

---

`setBounds` is broken in Phaser 3.

### --feedback--

It works correctly. The likely cause is call ordering.

---

You forgot to call `cameras.main.fixedToCamera = true`.

### --feedback--

There is no such property. Camera bounds is the right knob.

---

Camera bounds only work on the second-defined camera.

### --feedback--

They work on any camera, including `cameras.main`.

## --text--

Which combination is the standard cinematic-camera setup?

## --answers--

`cameras.main.setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240).startFollow(player, true, 0.1, 0.1).setDeadzone(200, 100)`.

---

`cameras.main.startFollow(player).setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)` and nothing else.

### --feedback--

This works, but skipping lerp and deadzone produces a rigid follow. The cinematic feel needs both.

---

`cameras.main.setZoom(0.5).setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)` and nothing else.

### --feedback--

`setZoom` shrinks the world, but with no follow target the camera does not move with the player.

---

`cameras.main.flash(1000, 0, 0, 0).setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)`.

### --feedback--

`flash` is a transient color overlay, not part of basic camera setup.
