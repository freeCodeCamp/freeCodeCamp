# --quizzes--

## --quiz--

### --question--

#### --text--

What does `cameras.main.startFollow(this.player)` do?

#### --distractors--

Locks the player's position to `(0, 0)`.

---

Mirrors the player's velocity onto the camera.

---

Adds a second camera centred on the player.

#### --answer--

Tells the camera to centre itself on the player every frame, scrolling the world past on the canvas.

### --question--

#### --text--

`startFollow(target, true, 0.1, 0.1)` — what do the trailing `0.1` arguments do?

#### --distractors--

Set the camera's zoom to 10%.

---

Offset the camera by 10 pixels in each direction.

---

Trigger a 100 ms lerp animation on first follow.

#### --answer--

Smoothing (lerp) factors: each frame the camera moves 10% of the way toward the target's position, instead of snapping.

### --question--

#### --text--

What does `cameras.main.setDeadzone(80, 60)` do?

#### --distractors--

Hides the area `80 × 60` pixels from the player's view.

---

Sets the camera's render viewport to `80 × 60`.

---

Disables camera follow for `80–60` ms after a level transition.

#### --answer--

Defines a centred rectangle in screen space — while the follow target stays inside it, the camera does not move at all.

### --question--

#### --text--

What's the relationship between `physics.world.setBounds` and `cameras.main.setBounds`?

#### --distractors--

They're aliases — calling either one sets both.

---

`physics.world.setBounds` is for static bodies; `cameras.main.setBounds` is for dynamic bodies.

---

`cameras.main.setBounds` calls `physics.world.setBounds` internally.

#### --answer--

They're independent. `physics.world.setBounds` defines the simulation rectangle; `cameras.main.setBounds` clamps the camera's scroll. Most cinematic levels need both.

### --question--

#### --text--

You set `physics.world.setBounds(0, 0, 4000, 240)` but the player still walks straight off the right edge. What's missing?

#### --distractors--

`this.physics.start()` was never called.

---

`physics.world.setBoundsCollision(true, true, true, true)`.

---

The camera bounds were not set.

#### --answer--

`this.player.body.setCollideWorldBounds(true)` — bodies opt in to bounds clamping per-body, not globally.

### --question--

#### --text--

You set physics world bounds correctly but forget camera bounds in a 4000-pixel-wide level. The bug the player sees is:

#### --distractors--

The player can pass through walls.

---

Physics velocity feels sluggish.

---

The HUD overlaps the player.

#### --answer--

The camera scrolls past the right edge of the level, exposing empty space outside the world.

### --question--

#### --text--

`this.cameras.main.shake(200, 0.012)` — what does the second argument mean?

#### --distractors--

Total displacement in pixels.

---

Number of shake oscillations.

---

A flat coefficient applied to shake; same pixel displacement on every screen size.

#### --answer--

Intensity as a fraction of `(width + height) / 2`, so the *visible* shake scales with the viewport size.

### --question--

#### --text--

What's the difference between `flash` and `fade`?

#### --distractors--

`flash` only accepts white; `fade` accepts any RGB.

---

`flash` is for hit feedback only; `fade` is just an alias.

---

`fade` works only between scenes; `flash` works only inside one scene.

#### --answer--

`flash(duration, r, g, b)` paints the viewport in a colour that fades from full opacity to zero. `fade(duration, r, g, b)` fades **in** to a fully opaque overlay that stays — typically used for scene transitions.

### --question--

#### --text--

Which moment is the **right** time to add screen shake?

#### --distractors--

Continuously while the player is on fire.

---

While the player walks across a flat floor.

---

On every coin pickup so the score "feels heavy."

#### --answer--

On a discrete impact event — like the player taking a hit or a boss exploding — for 100–300 ms.

### --question--

#### --text--

You shake the main camera and the score text shakes with the world even though it has `setScrollFactor(0)`. Why?

#### --distractors--

`setScrollFactor` is broken on `Text`; use `setScrollFactor(0, 0)` with both axes.

---

A bug in Phaser 3.80; bumping the version fixes it.

---

The text was added before the score was set, so it inherited the world's transform.

#### --answer--

Scroll factor only blocks scroll; it does not block shake. Shake is applied at the camera level and affects every object that camera renders, including pinned ones.

### --question--

#### --text--

What does `this.cameras.main.ignore(this.hud)` do?

#### --distractors--

Disables input on the HUD.

---

Hides the HUD on every camera in the scene.

---

Removes the HUD from the scene.

#### --answer--

The main camera skips rendering the `hud` (and every member, if it's a group/container) — but other cameras still render it.

### --question--

#### --text--

You add a UI camera but forget `uiCam.ignore([worldObjects])`. What goes wrong?

#### --distractors--

The UI camera doesn't render anything at all.

---

Phaser throws "no objects to render."

---

The world becomes invisible.

#### --answer--

Every world object renders twice — once on the main camera, once on the UI camera — so the world appears doubled or scrolls "two layers" past the HUD.

### --question--

#### --text--

You have `cameras.main` and one `uiCam = cameras.add(...)`. Which renders on top?

#### --distractors--

`cameras.main` always renders on top — that's why it's "main."

---

The camera with the highest `depth` value.

---

Whichever camera the active scene targets.

#### --answer--

`uiCam`, because it was added after `cameras.main`. Cameras render in add order; later cameras paint on top.

### --question--

#### --text--

Inside the world (main) camera you set a particle emitter to `setDepth(1000)`. The HUD background sits at `setDepth(0)` on the UI camera. Which renders on top?

#### --distractors--

The particles, because their depth is 1000.

---

Whichever was added to the scene last.

---

Both — Phaser composites them.

#### --answer--

The HUD background. Depth is per-camera, and the UI camera draws after the main camera regardless of depth values inside it.

### --question--

#### --text--

What does `tilePositionX += 0.4` do on a `tileSprite`?

#### --distractors--

Moves the tile sprite 0.4 pixels right on the canvas.

---

Stretches the texture by 40%.

---

Adds a new tile to the right of the visible rectangle.

#### --answer--

Shifts the tiled texture inside the visible rectangle, which looks like the texture scrolling. The tile sprite's position on the canvas does not change.

### --question--

#### --text--

For an auto-scrolling endless runner where the player is anchored at a fixed X position, what's the cleanest way to animate parallax?

#### --distractors--

Use `cameras.main.startFollow(player)` and adjust scroll factors per layer.

---

Set the player's `setScrollFactor(1)` and the layers' `setScrollFactor(0)`.

---

Use `body.setVelocityX` on each `tileSprite`.

#### --answer--

Set every layer's `setScrollFactor(0)` and animate `tilePositionX` directly in `update`, multiplying by `delta / 1000` for frame-rate independence.

### --question--

#### --text--

You added a 5-layer parallax background, then added `cameras.main.startFollow(player)` for an unrelated debug feature. Now the layers move twice as fast. Why?

#### --distractors--

`tilePositionX` is being applied per-frame instead of per-second.

---

`startFollow` always doubles parallax speed.

---

Phaser cache bug; refresh the page.

#### --answer--

Both systems are scrolling: the camera scrolls because of `startFollow` (so layers with non-zero scroll factor move) **and** `tilePositionX` is incrementing in `update`. Pick one mechanism.

### --question--

#### --text--

In a parallax stack — sky, mountains, hills, ground — which layer should have the **slowest** scroll-factor or `tilePositionX` advance rate?

#### --distractors--

Ground — closest to the player, so it should feel anchored.

---

Mountains — they're an environmental layer, never moves.

---

All four should advance at the same rate.

#### --answer--

Sky. The farther away a layer feels, the slower it should scroll.
