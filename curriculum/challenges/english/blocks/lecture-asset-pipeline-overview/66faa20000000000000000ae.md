# --interactive--

Once your game has more than a handful of sprites, you have to make a choice: do you load every asset before the player sees anything, or do you load some lazily as scenes change? The answer depends on what your players' devices can do — and how patient they are.

Consider three loading strategies:

- **Eager (load everything at boot).** Your `preload` method fetches every sprite, audio file, and tilemap before the title screen appears. Pro: never a hitch mid-game. Con: long initial load. Works for small games (<5 MB total).
- **Per-scene (load only what this scene needs).** Each `Phaser.Scene`'s own `preload` fetches only its assets. Pro: fast first-screen load; large games stay playable. Con: brief loading screens between scenes; complex bookkeeping.
- **Streaming (lazy + cache).** Background tasks fetch and cache "soon-needed" assets while the current scene is playing. Pro: feels instant. Con: hard to get right and easy to break on flaky networks.

Most learner games — and every workshop in this certification — use the eager strategy. The total asset budget is small enough (under ~5 MB across the whole certification) that loading everything once costs less than 200 ms on a desktop and under 2 s on a 4G phone.

There are limits worth respecting:

- Mobile data plans punish multi-megabyte downloads. A 2024 mid-range Android phone on 4G fetches at 10–20 Mbit/s, but every byte you ship over cellular costs your player something. Aim for total game payload under 5 MB whenever possible.
- The browser cap on parallel HTTP requests is 6 per origin. Twelve small sprite sheets ship faster than one giant atlas only if your CDN can keep all 12 connections warm. Otherwise, fewer-larger files win.
- iOS Safari decompresses images on the main thread when the texture is first uploaded. Loading a 4096×4096 sprite sheet causes a visible jank spike on iPhone 8-class devices. Keep individual sheets under 2048×2048.

A practical rule of thumb for a workshop-scale Phaser game:

| Asset class             | Per-scene budget | Total game budget |
| ----------------------- | ---------------- | ----------------- |
| Sprite sheets / atlases | <500 KB          | <1.5 MB           |
| Tilemap PNGs            | <500 KB          | <1 MB             |
| Audio (SFX + music)     | <1 MB            | <2 MB             |

For Chapter 2's workshop, you'll load three sprite sheets totaling under 1 KB. That's not the budget you'll always have, but it's a reminder that pixel art's appeal is partly economic — a 16-color, 16×16 sprite weighs essentially nothing.

## --text--

Your game ships 4 MB of pixel art across 60 small PNGs. Which loading strategy is the best default?

## --answers--

Eager — load everything in the first scene's `preload`, since the total payload is small.

---

Streaming — fetch each PNG only when it first appears on screen.

### --feedback--

Streaming is overkill at this size and trades simplicity for marginal gain.

---

Per-scene — split the 60 PNGs across 60 scenes.

### --feedback--

That fragments the load across many tiny requests; eager is simpler at this size.

---

Don't load any of them; reference them by URL at draw time.

### --feedback--

Phaser doesn't draw from raw URLs; the texture has to be in its cache first.

Why is a single 4096×4096 sprite sheet a problem on older iPhones?

## --answers--

Safari decompresses the image on the main thread when the texture first uploads, causing a jank spike.

---

iOS does not support PNG above 2048 pixels.

### --feedback--

iOS supports much larger PNGs — the issue is the decompression cost, not the format.

---

The Phaser loader rejects images larger than 2048×2048.

### --feedback--

Phaser will load the image; the device, not the framework, is the bottleneck.

---

Browsers do not allow textures over 2048 pixels for security reasons.

### --feedback--

There is no security restriction on texture size.
