# --description--

User privacy is all about your right to control how your personal data is collected, used, and shared online. Modern privacy frameworks rely on several core building blocks to protect your digital footprint.

At the heart of privacy protection is **Personally Identifiable Information (PII).** PII is any data that can directly or indirectly pinpoint your identity.

Direct identifiers include your full name, home address, and national identification number, while indirect identifiers include your IP address, device serial number, or precise location history.

To keep this data safe, companies must maintain **Confidentiality**. Confidentiality ensures that your private data remains safe from unauthorized access through the use of strong encryption and access controls.

If an unauthorized third party reads your records or leaks your PII, your confidentiality has been broken.

Privacy frameworks enforce this through **Data Minimization** and **Purpose Limitation**. Data minimization states that companies should only collect the absolute minimum amount of PII required to complete a specific task.

Purpose limitation dictates that once a business collects your data for a specific reason, they cannot legally use it for anything else later on, such as selling your contact info to advertising networks.

These principles directly combat invasive digital tracking. **Tracking** is the continuous monitoring of your online movements across the internet.

Advertising networks use scripts, device fingerprinting, and browser history logs to trace your behavior and compile detailed profiles without your knowledge.

To protect against this tracking, user consent forms the bedrock of individual control. True privacy requires informed, explicit consent, meaning you must actively opt in to data tracking rather than being forced to do so.

Finally, **privacy laws guarantee the Right to be Forgotten,** also known as data erasure. This concept gives you the legal right to request that an organization permanently delete all your personal records and tracking history from its databases once you close your account.

You'll learn about those privacy laws and the bodies responsible for them in the next lecture.
