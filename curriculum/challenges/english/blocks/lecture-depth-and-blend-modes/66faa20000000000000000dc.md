# --interactive--

By default, every sprite Phaser draws uses the `NORMAL` blend mode: the new pixel replaces what was underneath, weighted by the new pixel's alpha. That's the right choice for 99% of game art — characters, UI, props.

The other 1% is where the visuals get interesting. **Blend modes** describe how a new pixel combines mathematically with the pixel already on the buffer. Phaser exposes them through `Phaser.BlendModes`:

- `NORMAL` — the default. New pixel over old pixel, alpha-weighted.
- `ADD` — new + old (clamped to 1.0). Light gets brighter where it overlaps. Use for laser beams, glow rings, explosion flashes, and anything that should look like emitted light.
- `MULTIPLY` — new × old. Areas darken. Use for shadows, screen-darkening filters, mood overlays.
- `SCREEN` — `1 - (1 - new) × (1 - old)`. The opposite of multiply: areas lighten without clipping as harshly as ADD. Use for soft glows and atmospheric haze.

You set a blend mode on any game object via `setBlendMode`:

```js
const ring = this.add.graphics();
ring.setBlendMode(Phaser.BlendModes.ADD);
ring.lineStyle(4, 0xffffff, 1);
ring.strokeCircle(this.player.x, this.player.y, 24);
```

That single line — `setBlendMode(Phaser.BlendModes.ADD)` — turns a flat white ring into a glowing, additive halo that brightens whatever's underneath.

Blend modes are not a free lunch:

- They only work in Phaser's **WebGL** renderer (the default). The Canvas fallback ignores anything beyond `NORMAL`. If you support Canvas, build the visual to look acceptable without the blend, then layer the blend as a bonus.
- Each blend-mode change is a render-state flush. Stacking dozens of `ADD`-blended particles is fine; randomly intermixing blends across hundreds of objects costs frame time.
- `ADD` clipped to 1.0 looks white. If your "glow" stops feeling glowy, the issue is usually that you've saturated the channels — back the alpha down, or thin out the source color, instead of stacking more layers.

The mental shortcut: `NORMAL` for opaque art, `ADD` for emitted light, `MULTIPLY` for absorbed light, `SCREEN` for diffuse soft light.

# --questions--

## --text--

Which blend mode would you set on a sprite that represents a glowing energy beam laid over a dark background?

## --answers--

`Phaser.BlendModes.ADD` — adds the beam's color to whatever's underneath, brightening it.

---

`Phaser.BlendModes.NORMAL`

### --feedback--

`NORMAL` would just paint the beam over the background like a sticker — no glow.

---

`Phaser.BlendModes.MULTIPLY`

### --feedback--

`MULTIPLY` darkens the result; that's the opposite of a glow.

---

`Phaser.BlendModes.ERASE`

### --feedback--

`ERASE` punches a hole through what's underneath — also not a glow.

## --text--

You configure your Phaser game with `type: Phaser.CANVAS`. Several sprites use `setBlendMode(Phaser.BlendModes.ADD)`. What happens?

## --answers--

The blend mode is silently ignored — Canvas only supports `NORMAL`, so the sprites paint flat.

---

Phaser throws a runtime error when you try to set the blend mode.

### --feedback--

Phaser doesn't throw — it just falls back to the default blend.

---

The sprites become invisible.

### --feedback--

They render normally; the blend just isn't applied.

---

The blend is approximated as `MULTIPLY` instead.

### --feedback--

Canvas does not approximate WebGL blend modes; it ignores them.

## --text--

A puddle of red `ADD`-blended ink is laid over white sand. The result looks white, not red. Why?

## --answers--

`ADD` clips at 1.0 per channel. White already saturates every channel, so adding red pushes everything to 1 and the result reads as white.

---

The puddle's depth is too low; it's hiding under the sand.

### --feedback--

Depth would hide the puddle; the result here is the *blend* result.

---

Phaser disables `ADD` over light backgrounds for accessibility.

### --feedback--

Phaser does no such thing.

---

`ADD` only works against pure black backgrounds.

### --feedback--

It works against any background, but bright backgrounds saturate quickly.
