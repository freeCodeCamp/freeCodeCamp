# --interactive--

A scene with five layers feels orderly. A scene with twenty depth values picked at random feels like spaghetti. The trick is to standardize on a **layering recipe** before any sprite hits the screen.

A recipe that scales from a coin collector to a full RPG:

| Layer       | Depth | Examples                                                  |
| ----------- | ----- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Background  | 0     | Sky, distant parallax mountains, room walls               |
| Midground   | 5     | Static decorations, ground tiles                          |
| Entities    | 10    | Player, enemies, pickups, projectiles                     |
| Foreground  | 50    | Trees in front, particle bursts, hit-flashes              |
| HUD overlay | 100   | Score text, lives icons, minimap                          |
| Modals      | 200   | Pause menus, dialogue boxes, "Level Cleared!" overlays    |

A few rules that keep it from drifting:

- **Leave gaps.** Background at 0, entities at 10, HUD at 100. When a contributor adds particles "above the player but below the HUD," there's room at depth 50 without renumbering.
- **Group similar things.** All entities at depth 10 (sorting by creation order is fine for them). All UI on a single overlay depth.
- **Document the recipe.** A short comment near the top of `create` is enough — `// depths: bg=0, mid=5, ent=10, fg=50, hud=100, modal=200`. Saves the next contributor from spelunking.

For HUD elements there's a second pattern that's stricter than depth alone: the **second camera**. Phaser scenes can host more than one camera, and a HUD camera can ignore the gameplay layer entirely while the gameplay camera ignores the HUD. That's the cleanest separation, and you'll wire it in the next workshop.

A common newbie mistake is to micromanage every depth. If you're writing `setDepth` inside a `forEach` loop, you're probably renumbering when you should be leaving everything in the entity layer. The recipe is meant to be fixed; per-instance overrides are exceptions.

A second mistake is putting tween callbacks at higher depths than the source. A "+10" floater that comes out of a coin should still be in the foreground (50), not on the HUD (100). HUD is reserved for things that move with the camera and never with the world.

If your scene starts looking unreadable, the answer is almost always to flatten the depth map back down to the recipe and let the camera + creation-order do the rest.

# --questions--

## --text--

Following the layering recipe — bg=0, mid=5, ent=10, fg=50, hud=100 — at which depth should you place a "+10" damage-number floater that pops out of an enemy?

## --answers--

50 — it's a foreground effect, not a HUD element.

---

100 — anything that's text should sit at the HUD layer.

### --feedback--

HUD is reserved for camera-locked overlays, not world-space effects.

---

5 — it's a small decoration.

### --feedback--

5 is for static midground decor; floaters move and need to read above entities.

---

0 — backgrounds are the safest default.

### --feedback--

A floater at 0 would render under the enemy itself.

## --text--

Why leave gaps between the conventional depths (0, 10, 50, 100) instead of using consecutive integers (0, 1, 2, 3)?

## --answers--

So a future layer can slot between two existing ones without renumbering everything.

---

Because Phaser only accepts depth values that are multiples of 5.

### --feedback--

Phaser accepts any number.

---

To hint to the GPU that depth values aren't critical.

### --feedback--

The GPU doesn't care; this is purely a maintenance choice.

---

Because consecutive integers are reserved for animation frames.

### --feedback--

Animation frame indices are unrelated to render depth.

## --text--

A teammate complains the pause menu sometimes flickers under a particle effect. Their depths: menu=10, particles=10. Which fix matches the recipe?

## --answers--

Move the menu to depth 200 (modal layer). Modals always sit above everything, including foreground effects.

---

Move the particles to depth 0.

### --feedback--

Particles still need to render above gameplay, just below modals.

---

Disable depth and rely on creation order.

### --feedback--

Creation order is exactly what's biting them; explicit depth is the fix.

---

Lower the framerate so the flicker becomes invisible.

### --feedback--

Framerate doesn't change the underlying depth ordering.
