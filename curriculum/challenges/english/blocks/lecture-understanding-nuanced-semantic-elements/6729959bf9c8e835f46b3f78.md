# --interactive--

LSTMs and GRUs were a major step forward for sequence modelling. But as researchers pushed them to handle longer sequences and larger datasets, fundamental limitations emerged. These limitations directly motivated the invention of the transformer.

### Problem 1: Sequential Processing Cannot Be Parallelised

An RNN processes one time step at a time. To compute the hidden state at step 50, it must first compute step 1, then step 2, all the way through step 49. Each step depends on the previous one.

This means RNNs cannot take advantage of modern GPU hardware, which excels at performing thousands of operations simultaneously. Training an RNN on a long sequence is inherently slow because the computation is chained — you cannot skip ahead.

In contrast, a transformer processes all positions in a sequence simultaneously. This makes transformers dramatically faster to train on modern hardware.

### Problem 2: Long-Range Dependencies Still Suffer

LSTMs improved on simple RNNs, but they still struggle with very long sequences. The hidden state at step 500 of a long document is a compressed, lossy summary of everything before it. Early information gets diluted.

Consider translating a long legal document. A key term defined in paragraph 1 might determine the meaning of a pronoun in paragraph 20. An RNN must carry that connection through hundreds of intermediate hidden states. A transformer can connect any two positions directly, regardless of distance.

### Problem 3: The Bottleneck of a Fixed-Size Hidden State

In sequence-to-sequence models (like machine translation), early RNN architectures compressed the entire input sequence into a single fixed-size hidden state vector, then used that to generate the output. This **information bottleneck** meant that long inputs were poorly translated because too much information had to be squeezed into one vector.

This problem was partially addressed by the **attention mechanism** (introduced in 2015 by Bahdanau et al.), which allowed the decoder to look back at all encoder hidden states rather than just the final one. Attention was so effective that researchers eventually asked: what if we removed the RNN entirely and used only attention?

### The Answer: Transformers

In 2017, the paper "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. introduced the transformer architecture. It replaced recurrent connections entirely with a mechanism called **self-attention**, which allows every position in a sequence to directly attend to every other position in a single step.

The result was a model that:
- Trains much faster due to full parallelisation
- Handles long-range dependencies directly without degradation
- Scales to billions of parameters on large datasets
- Became the foundation of GPT, BERT, T5, and every major modern language model

### Key Points

- RNNs process sequences step by step, which prevents parallelisation and makes training slow
- Even LSTMs struggle with very long-range dependencies due to lossy hidden state compression
- The fixed-size hidden state creates an information bottleneck in sequence-to-sequence tasks
- The attention mechanism was invented to address the bottleneck, and was so effective that the RNN was eventually removed entirely
- The 2017 transformer paper "Attention Is All You Need" introduced self-attention and transformed the field

Why can RNNs not take full advantage of modern GPU hardware?

## --answers--

GPUs do not support the floating point operations used in RNN weight matrices

### --feedback--

GPUs are specifically designed for floating point matrix operations — the exact operations RNNs use. The limitation is architectural, not a hardware incompatibility.

---

RNNs process one time step at a time sequentially, so each step must wait for the previous one to complete

---

RNNs require integer arithmetic, which GPUs cannot perform efficiently

### --feedback--

RNNs use floating point arithmetic, which GPUs handle very well. The parallelisation problem is about the sequential dependency between time steps.

---

GPUs only support convolutional operations, not recurrent ones

### --feedback--

GPUs support all types of matrix operations, including those in RNNs. The issue is that RNN computation is inherently sequential, not that GPUs lack support for it.

## --text--

What was the information bottleneck problem in early sequence-to-sequence RNN models?

## --answers--

The model could only process sequences shorter than 100 tokens due to memory limits

### --feedback--

The bottleneck is not a hard memory limit on sequence length. It is about how well information is preserved when compressed into a fixed-size representation.

---

The encoder compressed the entire input into one fixed-size vector, losing information from long inputs

---

The decoder could not generate output sequences longer than the input sequence

### --feedback--

Output length is a separate concern. The bottleneck refers to the single fixed-size vector the encoder produced to represent the entire input, regardless of how long it was.

---

Gradients could not flow from the decoder back to the encoder during training

### --feedback--

Gradient flow between encoder and decoder is possible in RNN seq2seq models. The bottleneck is about information capacity — squeezing a long input into a single fixed-size vector.

That deeper RNNs with more layers could overcome the vanishing gradient problem entirely

### --feedback--

Deeper RNNs still suffer from sequential computation and long-range dependency issues. The paper proposed something fundamentally different from making RNNs deeper.

### --feedback--

The paper replaced RNNs with a different mechanism. CNNs were not the proposed replacement — something new was introduced.

That recurrent connections could be replaced entirely with self-attention, enabling full parallelisation and direct long-range connections

Pre-training at scale came later with BERT and GPT. The paper's core contribution was the architectural innovation of replacing recurrence with self-attention.
