# --interactive--

The simplest jump check in a platformer is one line:

```js
if (justPressedJump && body.touching.down) player.setVelocityY(-360);
```

It works. It is also the single biggest reason a platformer feels stiff. Two specific failures hit beginners constantly:

**Failure 1 — leaving the ledge.** The player walks toward a gap, presses jump. By the time the input registers, the player has already stepped off the ledge for one or two frames. `body.touching.down` is now `false`. The jump silently does nothing. The player swears the game is broken. They were 30 ms late, and at 60 fps, 30 ms is two frames — invisible to the player but fatal to the check.

**Failure 2 — landing.** The player is falling toward a platform. They press jump 30 ms before they land. `body.touching.down` is `false` because they have not landed yet. The jump silently does nothing. They land. They press jump again, finally on the floor, and now they jump. But for that 30 ms window, the input was eaten.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function update() {
  const body = this.player.body;
  if (Phaser.Input.Keyboard.JustDown(this.keys.up) && body.touching.down) {
    this.player.setVelocityY(-360);
  }
}
```

:::

Both failures are about a mismatch between how the *player* perceives time and how the *engine* gates input. Players input intent — "I want to jump *now*-ish" — and the engine demands frame-perfect timing — "are you on the ground at this exact frame?" Most players will input intent within ±100 ms of the right moment. A naive check rejects everything outside the same physics tick.

The fix has a name: **input forgiveness**. We give the player a small grace window in two directions:

- **Coyote time** — accept jumps that come up to N ms *after* leaving the ground.
- **Jump buffering** — accept jumps that come up to N ms *before* landing.

Both are handled by remembering one timestamp and comparing it against `scene.time.now`. The next two lectures unpack each one.

# --questions--

## --text--

Which two specific player behaviors does naive `touching.down`-only jump rejection fail?

## --answers--

(1) Pressing jump just *after* stepping off a ledge, and (2) pressing jump just *before* landing on a platform.

---

(1) Pressing jump while moving left, and (2) pressing jump while moving right.

### --feedback--

Horizontal velocity does not affect a `touching.down` check.

---

(1) Pressing jump twice in the same frame, and (2) pressing jump while paused.

### --feedback--

Those are not the common naive-jump complaints.

---

(1) Holding jump and (2) tapping jump.

### --feedback--

Both produce the same result with a simple `JustDown` check — the issue is timing relative to the ground, not press style.

A typical "I pressed jump right at the edge" failure window is around how many milliseconds?

## --answers--

About 50–150 ms — invisible to the player, but multiple physics frames at 60 fps.

---

About 1–2 seconds — the player held jump for a long time.

### --feedback--

That's not a timing failure, that's holding the button.

---

Less than a single frame — sub-millisecond.

### --feedback--

Sub-frame inputs are caught by `JustDown`. The 30–100 ms range is the unforgiving zone.

---

About 5 minutes — the player paused and forgot.

### --feedback--

Way outside any "feels bad" timing.

## --text--

Why does naive `if (justPressed && touching.down) jump()` punish players who feel like they *did* press at the right moment?

## --answers--

Player intent is fuzzy in time (±100 ms) but the engine demands the press to happen on the exact frame the body is touching ground.

---

The engine is buggy and ignores some keypresses.

### --feedback--

The engine is doing exactly what you asked. The check is too strict.

---

Players physically press the button too softly.

### --feedback--

Modern keyboards register every press; this is a logic-side issue.

---

`JustDown` is unreliable across browsers.

### --feedback--

`JustDown` is reliable. The window of validity is what's wrong.
