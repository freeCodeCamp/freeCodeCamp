# --interactive--

A camera that snaps exactly onto the player feels rigid. The world appears to teleport every time the player changes direction. Real cameras (and good game cameras) lag slightly behind the action — and that small lag is what makes the motion feel cinematic.

Phaser bakes the lag into `startFollow` via two extra arguments — `lerpX` and `lerpY`:

```js
this.cameras.main.startFollow(this.player, true, 0.1, 0.1);
```

The third and fourth arguments are the **lerp factors** for the X and Y axes. The default is `1` (no smoothing — snap directly). `0.1` means the camera covers 10% of the remaining distance to the target each frame, so on a 60 fps display it takes roughly half a second to fully catch up.

**Linear interpolation** (lerp) is just a mix between two values. The formula is:

```js
next = current + (target - current) * t;
```

When `t = 1`, `next` is the target (instant). When `t = 0`, `next` is the current position (frozen). Anything in between produces an exponential approach.

:::interactive_editor

```js
let camera = 0;
const playerX = 100;
const lerp = 0.1;

const trace = [];
for (let frame = 0; frame < 30; frame++) {
  camera = camera + (playerX - camera) * lerp;
  trace.push(camera.toFixed(2));
}
console.log(trace.slice(0, 10).join(", "), "...");
console.log("after 30 frames:", camera.toFixed(2));
```

:::

Two practical rules:

- `0.05`–`0.15` is the sweet spot for general gameplay. `0.1` is a good default.
- Different lerp on each axis is sometimes useful. Platformers often use `lerpX: 0.1, lerpY: 1` so the camera tracks horizontal motion smoothly but snaps vertically when the player jumps — preventing the camera from drifting when the player ascends and descends rapidly.

Lerp does *not* slow the camera's eventual top speed. If the player keeps moving away faster than the camera can catch up, the gap widens until the player escapes the frame. For that, you combine lerp with a **deadzone** (next lecture).

You can adjust lerp at runtime with `cameras.main.setLerp(x, y)` if you want to swap between cinematic-cutscene smoothing and snappy gameplay smoothing without re-targeting.

# --questions--

## --text--

`cameras.main.startFollow(target, true, 0.1, 0.1)` — what does the `0.1` do?

## --answers--

The camera covers 10% of the remaining distance to the target each frame, producing a smooth trailing motion.

---

The camera moves at 10% of its normal speed.

### --feedback--

There is no notion of "normal speed" — the camera's speed depends on the gap between it and the target. Lerp is a per-frame fraction of that gap.

---

The camera snaps to the target every 10 frames.

### --feedback--

Lerp is a per-frame interpolation, not a periodic snap.

---

The camera ignores movements smaller than `0.1` pixels.

### --feedback--

That would be a deadzone. Lerp is about *speed*, deadzones are about *threshold*.

## --text--

A platformer has the player jumping rapidly. Which lerp setting feels best?

## --answers--

`startFollow(player, true, 0.1, 1)` — smooth horizontal follow but vertical snaps so the camera does not drift while the player ascends and descends.

---

`startFollow(player, true, 1, 0.1)` — snappy horizontal, smooth vertical.

### --feedback--

This is backwards. Vertical motion in a platformer is fast and reversing; smooth Y makes the camera lag when the player lands. Snappy Y feels more grounded.

---

`startFollow(player, true, 0.05, 0.05)` — extra-smooth on both axes.

### --feedback--

Extra-smooth Y in a platformer makes the camera drift up while the player is rising and continue rising after the player lands. Most platformer cameras snap on Y.

---

`startFollow(player)` — defaults are tuned for platformers.

### --feedback--

Defaults are `lerp = 1` on both axes — exact tracking. Cinematic platformer cameras use lerp on X.

## --text--

You want to smoothly approach the target by 10% of the remaining distance each frame. Which formula matches that?

## --answers--

`camera = camera + (target - camera) * 0.1`

---

`camera = camera + 0.1`

### --feedback--

That moves the camera at a constant rate regardless of distance.

---

`camera = target * 0.1`

### --feedback--

That places the camera at 10% of the target position, which has nothing to do with distance.

---

`camera = camera * 0.9 + target`

### --feedback--

That overshoots — every frame the camera ends up roughly at `target`, then slightly more.
