# --interactive--

The most common platformer bug a new developer ships is "the player feels like it's getting clipped on edges." The cause is almost always the same: the physics body is the same size as the sprite, and the sprite has a couple of empty pixels around the visible art that nobody noticed.

Arcade physics gives you `body.setSize(width, height)` and `body.setOffset(x, y)` to detach the body from the sprite. The sprite stays the same size for rendering, the body becomes whatever you say. This is *the* lever for tuning game feel.

```js
function create() {
  const player = this.physics.add.sprite(160, 120, "hero");
  player.body.setSize(10, 14);
  player.body.setOffset(3, 2);
}
```

:::interactive_editor

```js
function create() {
  const player = this.physics.add.sprite(160, 120, "hero");
  player.body.setSize(10, 14);
  player.body.setOffset(3, 2);

  this.platforms = this.physics.add.staticGroup();
  this.platforms.create(160, 200, "tile");
  this.physics.add.collider(player, this.platforms);
}
```

:::

Two common moves in real platformers:

1. **Smaller body than sprite** — gives the player a visual "fudge factor" so they squeeze through gaps that look impossibly tight. Mario does this. Celeste does this. Every platformer that feels good does this.
2. **Wider body, narrower top** — tune horizontal collision aggressively (so corners feel forgiving) without altering the vertical stand-on-platforms feel. You typically do this with two bodies on one entity, or by leaning on `setSize` + careful tile spacing.

`setOffset` is in the sprite's local space, measured from the top-left of the sprite frame. So a 16×16 sprite with a 10×14 body that should sit centered uses `setOffset(3, 2)` — 3 pixels of empty space on each horizontal edge, 2 pixels of clearance on top, and the body's bottom aligns with the sprite's bottom.

The trap to watch for: if you change the body size mid-flight (e.g., a "crouch"), you also need to fix the offset, otherwise the body slides up or down inside the sprite and the player visibly hovers or sinks.

# --questions--

## --text--

You set `body.setSize(10, 14)` on a 16×16 sprite. What does `body.setOffset(3, 2)` do?

## --answers--

Positions the body 3 px from the sprite's left edge and 2 px from its top edge — so a 10×14 body sits centered horizontally and bottom-aligned (almost) inside a 16×16 sprite.

---

Moves the entire sprite 3 px right and 2 px down.

### --feedback--

`setOffset` only moves the body relative to the sprite — the sprite itself does not move.

---

Adds 3 px of padding to each side of the body.

### --feedback--

It is an absolute offset from the sprite's top-left, not symmetric padding.

---

Sets the bounce values to `(3, 2)`.

### --feedback--

Bounce uses `setBounce`, not `setOffset`.

## --text--

The player's sprite is 16×16 pixels but the *visible* character only occupies the central 10×14. With no `setSize`/`setOffset`, what bug do players report?

## --answers--

The player gets caught on edges and corners that look like clear space.

---

The player phases through walls.

### --feedback--

Bigger body means *more* collision, not less.

---

The sprite renders at the wrong size.

### --feedback--

Body changes do not affect rendering.

---

The player floats above the floor.

### --feedback--

The body still ends at the same bottom edge — no vertical issue.

## --text--

Why is shrinking the player's hitbox below the sprite a near-universal pattern in good platformers?

## --answers--

It gives the player a small "forgiveness" margin so jumps that look like they should clear actually clear — players read the visual edge, not the body edge.

---

It improves collision performance.

### --feedback--

Body size does not affect Arcade physics performance meaningfully.

---

It allows the player to fit through diagonal corners.

### --feedback--

Arcade bodies are axis-aligned rectangles — there are no diagonal corners.

---

It enables one-way platforms.

### --feedback--

One-way platforms come from a process callback, not body size.
