# --interactive--

A one-way platform is a tile the player can jump *up* through but lands *on*. It is the foundation of platformer level design â€” every Mario, every Celeste, every Hollow Knight uses them constantly. The implementation is one extra parameter on `physics.add.collider`: the **process callback**.

`physics.add.collider(player, platforms, collide, processCallback)` runs `processCallback` *before* it resolves the overlap and decides whether to apply collision response. Return `true` to let the collision resolve normally; return `false` to ignore it for this frame. That is the entire trick.

```js
this.physics.add.collider(player, oneWayPlatforms, null, (player, platform) => {
  return player.body.bottom <= platform.body.top + 2;
});
```

:::interactive_editor

```js
this.physics.add.collider(this.player, this.oneWayPlatforms, null, (player, platform) => {
  return player.body.bottom <= platform.body.top + 2;
});
```

:::

The condition: collide only when the player's bottom is at or above the platform's top. If the player is jumping up through the platform, the bottom of the body is *below* the top of the platform, so the callback returns `false` and the collision is ignored â€” the player passes through. As soon as the player falls back down past the top, the next physics step has the player's bottom on the platform's top, the callback returns `true`, and the player lands.

The `+2` slack matters. Without it, a player whose body bottom is exactly equal to the platform top can flicker between collide and pass-through depending on rounding. Two pixels of tolerance kills the flicker without making the platform feel weird.

What this **doesn't** do: prevent the player from dropping back down. Most platformer designers want a "drop through" gesture (DOWN + JUMP, say) to fall through one-way platforms intentionally. That is also process-callback territory: also return `false` if the player is pressing DOWN. We will ship that pattern in step 33 of the workshop.

## --text--

Why do most one-way-platform implementations use `player.body.bottom <= platform.body.top + 2` instead of `player.body.bottom <= platform.body.top`?

## --answers--

The 2-pixel slack prevents flicker between "collide" and "pass through" when the bottom equals the top exactly due to floating-point rounding.

---

Because Phaser bodies are 2 pixels larger than they appear.

### --feedback--

Bodies are exactly the size you give them.

---

Because the world is 2 pixels offset from origin.

### --feedback--

The world starts at `(0, 0)` unless you change `physics.world.bounds`.

---

To compensate for the player's `setOffset`.

### --feedback--

`setOffset` is already accounted for in `body.bottom`.
