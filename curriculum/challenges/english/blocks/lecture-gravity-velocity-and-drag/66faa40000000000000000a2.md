# --interactive--

Three numbers describe how a body moves: velocity, acceleration, and drag. Phaser measures them all per second, so they compose cleanly with the engine's frame rate.

**Velocity** is the body's current speed in pixels per second. `body.setVelocityX(120)` means "move 120 pixels to the right every second." Phaser handles the per-frame math — you do not multiply by `delta` yourself.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function create() {
  const arrow = this.add.rectangle(60, 90, 32, 4, 0xffeeaa);
  this.physics.add.existing(arrow);
  arrow.body.setVelocityX(120);
}
```

:::

**Acceleration** is how fast velocity changes, in pixels per second per second (px/s²). `body.setAccelerationX(60)` means "increase X velocity by 60 px/s every second." After one second the body is moving 60 px/s; after two seconds, 120 px/s. World gravity is acceleration applied automatically every frame.

**Drag** is the opposite of acceleration: a constant deceleration applied per second when no acceleration is active in that direction. `body.setDragX(80)` shaves 80 px/s off the body's horizontal speed each second, easing motion to a stop without you having to write a single line of update code.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function create() {
  const cube = this.add.rectangle(60, 90, 16, 16, 0x88ccff);
  this.physics.add.existing(cube);
  cube.body.setVelocityX(200);
  cube.body.setDragX(80);
}
```

:::

Drag and acceleration apply simultaneously. Hold "right" → set acceleration → body speeds up. Release "right" → set acceleration to `0`, drag takes over → body coasts to a stop. That two-line pattern (`setAcceleration`/`setDrag`) is the entire foundation of arcade-feeling player movement; the platformer workshop in the next sprint leans on it heavily.

The mental model: velocity is *where you are headed right now*; acceleration is *how the engine will change that*; drag is *how the engine will undo it when nothing else is pushing*.

# --questions--

## --text--

A body has `setVelocityX(100)` set and no acceleration. After one second of game time, where is it relative to its start?

## --answers--

100 pixels to the right.

---

100 pixels regardless of direction — Phaser picks the axis at runtime.

### --feedback--

`setVelocityX` is X-axis only. Direction is decided by the sign.

---

Roughly `100 * delta` pixels — you have to multiply yourself.

### --feedback--

Phaser already integrates velocity per frame; the units are px/s, not px/frame.

---

It depends on the frame rate.

### --feedback--

That's exactly what `setVelocity` is designed to abstract over.

## --text--

A body has `setAccelerationY(50)`. It starts at rest. What is its Y velocity after two seconds?

## --answers--

`100` px/s.

---

`50` px/s — acceleration sets velocity directly.

### --feedback--

Acceleration is the *rate of change* of velocity, not a one-shot push.

---

`25` px/s — acceleration halves over time.

### --feedback--

Acceleration is constant unless you change it.

---

`0` px/s — acceleration cancels itself out.

### --feedback--

Cancellation only happens with drag, not acceleration.

## --text--

A body has `setVelocityX(200)` and `setDragX(50)`, with no acceleration. What happens over time?

## --answers--

The body decelerates at 50 px/s² and slows toward `0`.

---

The body's velocity stays at `200` forever.

### --feedback--

Drag actively reduces speed when no acceleration opposes it.

---

The body accelerates because drag and velocity are added together.

### --feedback--

Drag opposes motion; it does not add to velocity.

---

The body's drag is overridden by velocity.

### --feedback--

`setVelocity` and `setDrag` are independent settings, not competing ones.
