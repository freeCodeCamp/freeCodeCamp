# --interactive--

Juice that fights gameplay loses. A perfect-feeling hit on the wrong frame still ruins the run if it caused the player to mash through a parry window. The discipline:

- **Scale juice by impact magnitude.** A small hit gets a small stack; a critical hit gets the full stack plus extras. Don't shake the camera ±0.005 for tapping a coin.
- **Honour `prefers-reduced-motion`.** Some users physically can't watch heavy shake/flash. The OS tells you so via `window.matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)')`. Drop your juice multipliers to ~0.2 when set.
- **Cap concurrent juice.** When 5 enemies die on the same frame, do NOT fire 5 full-stack effects. Run one global "frame juice budget" — share a single shake instead of stacking 5.
- **Camera shake only on contact, not on misses.** A swing that whiffs should NOT shake the screen. Reserve shake for the on-hit moment.
- **Test on a phone.** Effects that look great on a 34" desktop monitor become nausea-inducing on a 6" phone held 30cm from your face.

The single multiplier pattern:

```js
class MainScene extends Phaser.Scene {
  create() {
    const reduced =
      window.matchMedia &&
      window.matchMedia("(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)").matches;
    this.juice = reduced ? 0.2 : 1;
  }

  onHit(enemy, magnitude) {
    const j = this.juice * magnitude;
    this.cameras.main.shake(80 * j, 0.005 * j);
    this.tweens.add({
      targets: enemy,
      scaleX: 1 + 0.3 * j,
      scaleY: 1 + 0.3 * j,
      duration: 60,
      yoyo: true
    });
    this.physics.world.pause();
    this.time.delayedCall(40 * j, () => this.physics.world.resume());
  }
}
```

`this.juice` becomes the master volume knob for every juice effect. With `magnitude = 1` for a regular hit, `magnitude = 2.5` for a heavy/crit hit, and `this.juice = 0.2` when reduced-motion is on, you cleanly cover four cases (regular hit, heavy hit, regular hit reduced, heavy hit reduced) with one expression each.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function effectiveShake({ baseAmplitude, magnitude, reduced }) {
  const juice = reduced ? 0.2 : 1;
  const result = baseAmplitude * magnitude * juice;
  return { magnitude, reduced, finalAmplitude: result };
}

console.log(
  "regular hit (motion-on)    :",
  effectiveShake({ baseAmplitude: 0.005, magnitude: 1, reduced: false })
);
console.log(
  "heavy hit (motion-on)      :",
  effectiveShake({ baseAmplitude: 0.005, magnitude: 2.5, reduced: false })
);
console.log(
  "regular hit (motion-reduced):",
  effectiveShake({ baseAmplitude: 0.005, magnitude: 1, reduced: true })
);
console.log(
  "heavy hit (motion-reduced)  :",
  effectiveShake({ baseAmplitude: 0.005, magnitude: 2.5, reduced: true })
);
```

:::

Notice that even a heavy hit with reduced motion still gives a slightly stronger signal than a regular hit with reduced motion (`0.0025` vs `0.001`) — the relative weighting between hits is preserved. That's the win: motion-reduced players still understand which hits are heavier, just with much smaller absolute amplitudes.

A common trap: making `prefers-reduced-motion` flip to `juice = 0`. That removes ALL feedback, leaving the player guessing if their attack landed. Always keep some multiplier (we use `0.2`) so the juice still does its job, just quieter.

Another trap: scaling shake but not the duration. A 1-frame `shake(20, 0.001)` is invisible. Either keep duration constant (~80ms) and only scale amplitude, or scale both — but never scale the duration to "near zero" because the shake disappears entirely.

## --text--

A user with `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` sets your juice multiplier to `0`. What is the actual problem this creates for them?

## --answers--

Total opt-out removes every feedback signal — they can't tell if their attack landed at all. The fix is to keep a small (~0.2) multiplier so the juice still confirms the hit, just much quieter.

## --text--

Five enemies die on the exact same frame in your brawler. Why is "fire 5 full-stack effects" the wrong move?

## --answers--

5 stacked shakes amplitude-add into nausea-inducing camera throw, 5 simultaneous hit-pauses freeze the world for 5×40ms = 200ms (stuttery), and 5 audio plays cause harsh peak clipping. The fix is a per-frame "juice budget": queue at most ONE full shake and ONE hit-pause per frame regardless of hit count.

---

Phaser crashes when more than 4 tweens fire on one frame.

### --feedback--

It handles many tweens fine.

---

The audio player only allows 2 sounds at once.

### --feedback--

Web Audio handles tens of concurrent sounds without trouble.

---

Particles can't render more than 3 emitters at once.

### --feedback--

Many emitters render fine.

## --text--

You scale `shake` amplitude with `magnitude` but keep duration fixed at 80ms. Heavy hits feel right; regular hits feel "nothing." Why?

## --answers--

You probably scaled amplitude all the way down for regular hits (`0.005 * 1 * 0.2 = 0.001`) — below the perceptual threshold. Either bump regular hits up (`0.003` minimum) or use a non-linear ramp like `Math.max(0.003, base * mag * juice)` so hits never drop into invisibility.

---

The shake duration must scale with amplitude.

### --feedback--

That's actually a worse pattern; tying duration to amplitude makes regular hits disappear entirely.

---

Phaser truncates shake amplitudes below 0.005.

### --feedback--

It does not.

---

The camera's frame-pacing is too slow at 60 FPS for short shakes.

### --feedback--

60 FPS is plenty for an 80ms shake; the issue is amplitude, not timing.
