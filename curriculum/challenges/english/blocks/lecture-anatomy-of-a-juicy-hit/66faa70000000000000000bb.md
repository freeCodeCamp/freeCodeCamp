# --interactive--

A "hit" in a game can be a single line of code: `enemy.destroy()`. Players hate that. The same hit, when six small effects fire on the same frame, *feels* like a meaningful blow. Layering effects so the brain reads "impact" is the core trick of game feel.

The six-stack recipe — drop any of these and the hit feels weaker; layer all six and the hit feels three times more powerful than its damage number suggests:

| Layer            | API                                                   | Duration | Job                                  |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | -------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Squash & stretch | `tweens.add({ scaleX: 1.3, yoyo: true, ... })`        | ~120ms   | Mass deformation under impact.       |
| Tint flash       | `setTint(0xffffff)` then `clearTint()`                | ~80ms    | "I felt that" frame.                 |
| Screen shake     | `cameras.main.shake(80, 0.005)`                       | ~80ms    | Impact transfers to the camera.      |
| Hit-pause        | `physics.world.pause()` + `time.delayedCall(40, ...)` | ~40ms    | Time stops to sell weight.           |
| Particle burst   | `add.particles(x, y, key, ...).explode(20)`           | ~600ms   | Debris spray — energy radiates out.  |
| Audio cue        | `sound.play('sfx-hit', { rate: 0.9..1.1 })`           | ~150ms   | The auditory exclamation point.      |

Each is short, but together they create a 600ms window where the player's eyes, ears, and proprioceptive sense all confirm the hit happened.

```js
onHit(hitbox, enemy) {
  this.tweens.add({
    targets: enemy,
    scaleX: 1.3,
    scaleY: 1.3,
    duration: 60,
    yoyo: true
  });
  enemy.setTint(0xffffff);
  this.time.delayedCall(80, () => enemy.clearTint());
  this.cameras.main.shake(80, 0.005);
  this.physics.world.pause();
  this.time.delayedCall(40, () => this.physics.world.resume());
  this.add
    .particles(enemy.x, enemy.y, "particle", { speed: 200, lifespan: 400, quantity: 1 })
    .explode(20);
  this.sound.play("sfx-hit", { rate: Phaser.Math.FloatBetween(0.9, 1.1) });
}
```

The detune trick (a random `rate` per play) prevents the SFX from sounding identical when the player chains hits — you'll add this in step 8 of the workshop.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function juiceCost(layers) {
  // approximate "perceived punch" weight of each effect, 1..3
  const weight = {
    squash: 2,
    tint: 1,
    shake: 3,
    hitPause: 3,
    particles: 2,
    audio: 2
  };
  let score = 0;
  for (const l of layers) score += weight[l] ?? 0;
  return score;
}

console.log("audio only      :", juiceCost(["audio"]));
console.log("audio + tint    :", juiceCost(["audio", "tint"]));
console.log("the full stack  :", juiceCost(["squash", "tint", "shake", "hitPause", "particles", "audio"]));
console.log("missing hitPause:", juiceCost(["squash", "tint", "shake", "particles", "audio"]));
```

:::

The intuition: a hit with audio only scores `2`. The full stack scores `13`. The missing-hit-pause variant scores `10`. There's a non-linear payoff: the difference between "good" and "great" feel is one or two layers, but the difference between "weak" and "good" is the *first* couple. Don't ship games with audio-only hits.

A common mistake is to make every effect long. A 600ms hit is roughly the upper bound for 60 FPS combat — much longer and the next hit overlaps awkwardly. Keep each layer short; let *layering* do the work, not duration.

# --questions--

## --text--

You ship a hit with audio + particles only. Why does it feel weaker than a competitor's hit with the full six-layer stack?

## --answers--

The brain confirms an impact through multiple sensory channels (visual deformation + camera shake + tactile pause + audio + spatial particles). Two channels alone is below the threshold where the brain reads "real impact."

---

The two-effect version uses the wrong audio file.

### --feedback--

Audio quality matters, but the same SFX with the full stack still feels punchier than with two effects. The win is in layering.

---

Phaser's renderer can't display particles and audio together at 60 FPS.

### --feedback--

It can. Performance is rarely the issue. The issue is sensory layering.

---

Two-effect hits are louder.

### --feedback--

Loudness has nothing to do with it; the brain reads "impact" from the *number* of synchronized signals, not the volume of any one of them.

## --text--

Which two layers are most often skipped in the full stack and why?

## --answers--

Hit-pause and screen shake — they're the most "invasive" (they affect global state), so devs skip them out of fear of motion sickness or weirdness on multi-enemy hits. Adding them is the single biggest "feel" upgrade.

---

Particles and audio — they require external assets.

### --feedback--

Particles are easy in 3.60+ (no asset required if you generate a texture in `BootScene`), and audio is in every game. They aren't the skipped ones.

---

Squash/stretch and tint flash — they require sprite-sheet authoring.

### --feedback--

Both work on any sprite via `setScale` and `setTint`. No asset authoring needed. They're rarely skipped.

---

All six are skipped equally.

### --feedback--

Empirically, hit-pause and screen shake are the most often left out. Worth knowing.

## --text--

Why does the full stack target ~600ms total instead of, say, 1500ms?

## --answers--

In 60-FPS combat, hits chain every ~300–600ms. A juice stack longer than that makes consecutive hits visually overlap and "muddy" — the player can't tell where one hit ended and the next began. Short, synchronized layers preserve combo legibility.

---

Browsers cap tween duration at 600ms.

### --feedback--

There is no such cap.

---

Above 600ms the audio stops looping.

### --feedback--

`sound.play` doesn't loop unless told to; the duration is unrelated.

---

Phaser's `tweens.add` ignores durations longer than 600ms.

### --feedback--

It honors any positive duration.
