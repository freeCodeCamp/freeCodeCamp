# --interactive--

Diffusion models are built on a beautifully simple idea: if you can teach a neural network to remove a small amount of noise from an image, you can run that process repeatedly — starting from pure noise — to generate a completely new image.

### The Problem with GANs

Before understanding why diffusion models work, it helps to understand why GANs have limitations. GANs require simultaneously training two competing networks. The training process is unstable, prone to mode collapse, and difficult to scale. GANs also provide no way to estimate how likely a generated sample is — you cannot ask "how realistic is this image?" in a principled way.

Diffusion models sidestep all of these issues by framing generation as a denoising problem.

### The Analogy: Restoring a Damaged Photograph

Imagine you have a clear photograph. You photocopy it, introducing a tiny amount of graininess. You photocopy the copy, adding more graininess. Repeat this hundreds of times and you eventually have a page of pure static — the original image is completely unrecognisable.

Now imagine learning to reverse this process. Given a slightly grainy image, predict what the cleaner version looked like. This is a much easier problem than generating an image from scratch — the noisy image already contains most of the information, and the model just needs to clean it up slightly.

Diffusion models do exactly this. They train a neural network to perform one small denoising step. To generate a new image, they start from pure random noise and apply that denoising step hundreds of times, progressively revealing a coherent image.

### Why This Works

The key insight is that the space of "slightly less noisy images" is much easier to learn than the space of "all realistic images". At each step, the model only needs to make a small local correction. Over many steps, these small corrections accumulate into a fully formed image.

This is analogous to how sculptors work: rather than conjuring the final sculpture all at once, they start with a rough block and make progressively finer refinements until the detail emerges.

### Two Processes

Every diffusion model involves two processes:

**Forward process (diffusion)** — gradually add noise to a real image over T steps until it becomes pure Gaussian noise. This process is fixed — it is not learned. It is a mathematical formula.

**Reverse process (denoising)** — learn to undo each noise step. A neural network is trained to predict what the image looked like one step earlier, given the current noisy version. This is what the model learns.

At generation time, only the reverse process is used: start from pure noise and repeatedly apply the learned denoising step.

### Comparison with GANs

| | GAN | Diffusion Model |
|---|---|---|
| Generation mechanism | Single forward pass through generator | Hundreds of iterative denoising steps |
| Training stability | Unstable — two competing networks | Stable — single denoising objective |
| Sample quality | High but can have artefacts | State of the art |
| Generation speed | Very fast (milliseconds) | Slower (seconds to minutes) |
| Mode coverage | Can suffer mode collapse | Covers full data distribution |
| Controllability | Conditional GANs required | Text conditioning built in naturally |

### Key Points

- Diffusion models frame image generation as an iterative denoising problem
- The forward process adds noise step by step until the image becomes pure noise — this is fixed and not learned
- The reverse process is learned: a neural network predicts how to remove a small amount of noise at each step
- Generation works by starting from pure noise and applying the learned denoising step hundreds of times
- Diffusion models are more stable to train than GANs and achieve state-of-the-art image quality

What is the core task a diffusion model's neural network learns to perform?

## --answers--

Generate a complete image from a single random noise vector in one step

### --feedback--

Generating a complete image in one step describes how a GAN generator works. Diffusion models use a fundamentally different iterative approach.

---

Remove a small amount of noise from a slightly noisy image to produce a slightly cleaner version

---

Classify whether an image is real or generated

### --feedback--

Classifying real vs generated images describes the GAN discriminator. The diffusion model's network learns a very different task.

---

Compress a real image into a compact latent representation

### --feedback--

Image compression describes an autoencoder's encoder. The diffusion model's key learned task is about noise, not compression.

What is the forward process in a diffusion model?

The neural network's forward pass during inference

### --feedback--

The neural network's forward pass is a standard deep learning concept. The forward process in diffusion models refers to something specific about how the training data is prepared.

---

The process of gradually adding noise to a real image over many steps until it becomes pure noise

---

The process of generating a new image by starting from noise and denoising it

### --feedback--

Starting from noise and denoising describes the reverse process — the one used at generation time. The forward process moves in the opposite direction.

---

The process of fine-tuning a pre-trained model on a new dataset

### --feedback--

Fine-tuning is a transfer learning technique. The forward process is specific to diffusion models and describes how noise is progressively added to training images.

## --text--

What is one key advantage of diffusion models over GANs?

## --answers--

Diffusion models generate images much faster than GANs

### --feedback--

This is actually the opposite — diffusion models are significantly slower than GANs because they require hundreds of iterative denoising steps. Their advantages lie elsewhere.

---

Diffusion models require less training data than GANs

### --feedback--

Diffusion models typically require large amounts of training data, similar to or more than GANs. Data requirements are not their advantage.

---

Diffusion models have a more stable training objective because they train a single denoising network rather than two competing networks

---

Diffusion models cannot be misused to generate harmful content

### --feedback--

Like all generative models, diffusion models can be misused. Stability and quality are their technical advantages over GANs, not immunity to misuse.
