# --interactive--

Real platformers do not place each platform pixel-by-pixel. They snap geometry to a grid — almost always 16, 24, or 32 pixels per tile — and lay terrain down a tile at a time. That single decision unlocks everything that follows: tilesheets, tilemap editors, jump-height tuning, even level generation.

The cheapest thing you can do is loop:

```js
const TILE = 16;
for (let x = 0; x < 320; x += TILE) {
  this.platforms.create(x + TILE / 2, 200, "tile");
}
```

That carpets a row of floor across the screen with one line of intent. To stack a wall, loop on `y` instead. To carve a pit, skip a range of `x`. To build stairs, increment `y` whenever `x` reaches a threshold. The grid lets you reason about the level in tile units, not pixels — when you say "the gap is three tiles wide and the player can jump four," that is a statement about the grid, not screen coordinates.

:::interactive_editor

```js
function create() {
  this.platforms = this.physics.add.staticGroup();
  const TILE = 16;
  for (let x = 0; x < 320; x += TILE) {
    if (x >= 96 && x < 144) continue;
    this.platforms.create(x + TILE / 2, 200, "tile");
  }
}
```

:::

Why 16 pixels? It traces back to the NES era — most retro art is drawn at 16×16 or 8×8, and modern game-feel research keeps confirming that bigger tiles read more clearly at common zoom levels. Pick a tile size early and stick with it through the whole project. Every platform sprite, hitbox, and physics constant is going to refer to it.

The other reason the grid matters: jump height. If your tile is 16 px and you want the player to clear two tiles, you need a jump height of about 32 px plus a safety margin. That gives you concrete numbers to plug into `setVelocityY` instead of guessing — we will derive these in the platformer workshop.

# --questions--

## --text--

You want a row of floor tiles spanning x = 0 to x = 320 with a 3-tile-wide pit between x = 96 and x = 144. Tile size is 16 px. Which approach is the cleanest?

## --answers--

Loop `x` from 0 to 320 in steps of 16, and `continue` when `x` is in the pit range.

---

Place each tile by hand with one `create` call per tile.

### --feedback--

Works for 4 tiles, falls apart at 40 tiles.

---

Create one giant rectangle at `x = 160` and remove the middle pixels with `setMask`.

### --feedback--

Visual masks do not change the physics body — the player would still walk on the masked region.

---

Use a tween to slide a single tile back and forth across the row.

### --feedback--

That gives you one tile, not a floor.

## --text--

Why do most 2D platformers pick a power-of-two tile size like 16 or 32?

## --answers--

It plays nicely with retro pixel art, scales evenly at 2× / 4× / 8× zoom, and gives you round numbers for jump-height and gap-width math.

---

Phaser's physics engine refuses to collide with non-power-of-two bodies.

### --feedback--

Arcade physics does not care about your tile size at all.

---

WebGL textures must be a power of two.

### --feedback--

WebGL2 lifted that restriction; even WebGL1 only requires it for mipmapped textures.

---

JavaScript integer math is faster on powers of two.

### --feedback--

The JIT does not care, and the cost is negligible at the size of one game level.
